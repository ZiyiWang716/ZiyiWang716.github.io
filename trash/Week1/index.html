"We judge you."



"But we do not even want to see you."





INSPIRATION



        In John Berger's Ways of Seeing, Berger brought up the idea of how women were oftend depicted as nude to feed an appetite of their male spectators. The very same idea is portrayed in the painting Phryne before the Areopagus by French artist Jean-Léon Gérôme. The subject matter is Phryne, a legendary courtesan in ancient Greece who was put on trial for impiety. Phryne was acquitted after her defender Hypereides removed her robe and exposed her naked bosom, "to excite the pity of her judges by the sight of her beauty." The woman depicted is weak, solidary, and submissive in the scene, satisfying the men's complacency of their dominace over the women.


        So here, I changed the story by switching the positions of the male and female figures. The man is now judged by his nudity and the women become sitting at the seats. However, the female judges do not even want to get a look at the body of the man. They are given the power to judge, but they just do not have an interest to do so.
